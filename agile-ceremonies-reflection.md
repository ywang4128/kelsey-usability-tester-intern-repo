# Understanding Agile Ceremonies & QA’s Role #42
## What are the four main Scrum ceremonies (Sprint Planning, Daily Standup, Sprint Review, Retrospective)?
The four main Scrum ceremonies are:
- Sprint Planning
- Daily Standup
- Sprint Review
- Sprint Retrospective

They give the team a regular rhythm to plan, inspect progress, review the product, and improve how they work.

## What happens in each ceremony, and who participates?
Sprint Planning
The team decides what to work on in the next sprint and how to do it. The Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Development Team (including QA) plan stories, set the sprint goal, and estimate effort.

Daily Standup
A short check‑in where everyone shares what they did yesterday, what they’ll do today, and any blockers. Attended by all team members, including QA.

Sprint Review
The team shows the completed work to stakeholders to get feedback. The Scrum Team + key stakeholders attend.

Sprint Retrospective
The team reflects on the sprint, discussing what went well, what didn’t, and how to improve. The Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers, QA) discusses process changes, including testing.

## What is QA’s role in each meeting?
Sprint Planning
QA helps clarify acceptance criteria, asks questions about edge cases, and estimates how much testing each story will need. QA can also flag risky or hard‑to‑test items upfront.

Daily Standup
QA shares:
What testing was done yesterday.
What will be tested today.
Any blockers.

Sprint Review
QA verifies that shown features are stable and bug‑free, and may demo or support the demo of key flows. QA can also point out known issues or known limitations.

Sprint Retrospective
QA talks about bugs that slipped, testing bottlenecks, and process issues and suggests concrete improvements.

## How can testers use Sprint Planning to prepare for upcoming work?
In Sprint Planning, QA can:
- Read user stories in advance and prepare questions about acceptance, edge cases, and testability.
- Think about what data, environments, and automation might be needed for the sprint.
- Suggest which areas are high‑risk so extra time or buffer is planned.

## Why is the Sprint Retrospective important for improving QA processes?
The Sprint Retrospective is where QA can:
- Highlight testing bottlenecks, late handoffs, or missing acceptance criteria.
- Propose actions like earlier QA involvement, better definition‑of‑done, or extra automation.
- Track whether previous QA‑related changes actually improved quality or speed.

## What should QA do before, during, and after a Sprint Planning meeting?
Before:
- Review the likely backlog items and note risks, missing acceptance criteria, and edge cases.
- Think about test data, environments, and any automation needs.

During:
- Ask questions about “Done” criteria and what needs to be tested.
- Estimate testing effort and point out any blockers.

After:
- Finalize a testing plan for the sprint.
- Update or create test cases and start automation or manual scripts early.

## If a Daily Standup is running long, what’s the best way to keep QA updates concise?
If standups run long, QA can keep it short and useful by:
- Sticking to 3 bullets max: yesterday, today, blocker
- Avoiding detailed bug explanations, and focusing on status and impact.

## How can QA drive continuous improvement during Sprint Retrospectives?
QA can drive improvement by:
- Sharing concrete examples.
- Suggesting small, actionable changes.
- Tracking the impact of those changes over a few sprints to see if bugs, testing time, or delays improve.
